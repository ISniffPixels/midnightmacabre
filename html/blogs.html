Top 10 Scariest Horror Films of All Time

By Staff

Everyone loves a good horror movie. From the '80s slasher craze to today's found-footage films, horror movies are endlessly popular with the general public. Horror films can be an extremely effective way to scare an audience, and they don't have to rely on cheap tricks or jump scares to do it. While there are many films that try and fail miserably at scaring us, there are others that succeed so well that we'll never forget them. Here are some of the scariest films ever made. Some of these creepy films will keep you awake at night - others will give you nightmares for years to come...

1. The Shining

First up on our list of the scariest horror films ever is The Shining. This 1980 movie, based on the novel by Stephen King, follows the story of Jack Torrance, a recovering alcoholic who agrees to become the caretaker of a remote hotel in Colorado for the winter months. The hotel's past is a dark one, and Torrance's exploration of the building's history soon begins to affect him. The film is particularly scary because of the way that it plays with the viewer's expectations. The Shining is the kind of movie where you know that something bad is going to happen, but you don't know when or how. The Shining is also scary because of how real it feels in its portrayal of a family that is slowly falling apart. We'd highly recommend this movie to anyone who's looking for a good scare.

2. The Exorcist

The Exorcist is an iconic horror film that many people have seen, but few have actually finished. This film, first released in 1973, revolves around a young girl who is possessed by a malevolent supernatural entity. The girl's mother seeks the help of two priests to perform an exorcism and save her daughter from this dark force. Throughout the film, the priests fail to drive the evil spirit from the girl, but they also draw on the power of God to try to save themselves from the same fate. This film has a seriously dark and creepy feel to it, and it has been scaring people for close to 50 years. The Exorcist is a great real-life example of a horror movie that actually scares people.

3. A Nightmare on Elm Street

A Nightmare on Elm Street is one of the most iconic horror films ever made. It tells the story of a group of high school students who are hunted down and killed by an unknown man while they sleep. The only way to break free of this fate is to stay awake, but the kids soon learn that not everything can be fought while they are conscious. This film is genuinely scary because it manages to be both fantastical and believable at the same time. The fact that it is set in a normal American high school, with normal people, who have no idea that a supernatural killer is hunting them down, makes it feel all the more real. A Nightmare on Elm Street is a great example of how a low budget and unknown actors can still make an iconic horror film.

4. Halloween

Halloween is another classic horror movie that is still scaring people decades later. This film, which is actually the original version of a sequel, follows an escaped mental patient who stalks and murders a group of teenagers on Halloween night. Halloween actually started a whole new sub-genre within horror, known as the “slasher” genre. Many other films have followed in its footsteps, but few have done it as well as this original. Halloween is scary because it treats the death of children as normal. While most horror movies are about stopping a serial killer and saving the day, this film is almost like a documentary where the killer gets away. Halloween is the kind of film that will make you think long after the credits have rolled.

5. IT

IT is an adaptation of the 1986 Stephen King two-part novel of the same name. The setting takes place in Derry, Maine, where every 27 years, an ancient entity manifests into existence, and preys on the inner most fears of the town's children. A band of outcast children form The Loser's Club, and are the primary target of the trans-dimensional being, in a nightmarish saga, that takes the children on a mission to figure out what's happening in the town of Derry, Maine, and how it ties into the town's history. This movie is particularly unsettling, because it touches on conquering fear, but not without the risk of dying a terrifying death by the hands of a being, that is able to alter your reality, and leave you not only battling it, but also your own demons.

6. Dawn of the Dead

Dawn of the Dead comes from the zombie brains of director George A. Romero. This film, released in 1978, is actually a sequel to Romero's original “Night of the Living Dead”. This film is set in a world where a virus has turned a large portion of the population into zombie-like creatures, and a group of people have walled themselves up in a suburban shopping mall to survive. The film follows the group of survivors as they try to keep themselves safe both from the zombies outside the building and their own growing problems within. Dawn of the Dead is scary because it offers a grim and dark look at what happens when civilization breaks down. This is a world where the only people who survive are the ones who are strong enough to take what they need by force. It is a frightening look at what could happen to our society if we don't learn how to work together as a group.

7. Texas Chainsaw Massacre

The Texas Chainsaw Massacre is a truly iconic horror film that has inspired many other films and series, both on the big screen and on TV. This movie tells the story of a group of young people who drive out to a remote area and get trapped at a house run by a family of cannibals. The film is scary because it portrays the villains as truly beyond help. These are not people who are seeking redemption or who are trying to be good, these are evil people who are doing evil things because they are evil. This movie is less about the blood and gore of the murders and more about the dread that the viewer feels while watching it. The Texas Chainsaw Massacre uses jump scares and disturbing imagery to unsettle its audience, making them feel like they could be the ones sitting in the shoes of the unfortunate victims of the film.

8. The Babadook

The Babadook is a film that has gained a lot of popularity over the years, but there are still a lot of people who have missed it. This 2014 film follows a single mother and her young son as they try to overcome the strange things happening in their house, such as loud noises, a mysterious written word appearing on walls, and a creepy children's book that appears to have come to life. The Babadook is scary because it doesn't rely on jump scares or gore. Instead, it focuses on the mental stress that a single mother is under and the way that it can break her. This movie is genuinely creepy and unsettling, and it is the kind of film that will stay with you long after the credits have rolled. The Babadook is a great example of how a low budget and a new director can make an effective horror film.

9. Hellraiser

Hellraiser is one of the most popular horror franchises that people have never heard of. This film, released in 1987, tells the story of a man who is given a strange puzzle box that is tied to a mysterious evil force. The puzzle box is actually a doorway to another dimension, and it is tied to the fate of all of the world's sinners. The film is scary because it deals with an ancient evil that has been lurking in the shadows since the beginning of time. Its monstrous villain is motivated by the simple desire to gain more power, and the film is a reminder that even the most normal-looking people could actually be evil. The Hellraiser franchise is a great example of how a seemingly normal premise can be twisted into a nightmare-inducing film.

10. The Orphanage

The Orphanage is a truly chilling film that deserves more attention than it has gotten over the years. This movie is actually a Spanish film that was released in 2007, but it has become more and more popular in the years since. The Orphanage follows the story of a woman who returns to the orphanage where she grew up after the death of her adoptive mother. The film is scary because of its ability to draw on real emotion and mood. This movie makes you feel like something bad is going to happen, even when nothing seems amiss, and it leaves you feeling unsettled long after the credits have rolled. The Orphanage is a great example of how a low budget and a simple premise can be turned into an effective and memorable horror film.

The History of Halloween: A Celebration Of Fear

By Staff

Today, the month of October is more famous as the time for carving jack-o'-lanterns and binge-watching scary movies. But very few people are aware of the fascinating history behind this spooky festival of Halloween. The celebration of this mysterious festival has its roots in ancient cultural traditions and religious practices. However, the modern version of this spooky festival has transformed from a night of fright to a lighthearted holiday where kids go door-to-door trick-or-treating with costumed character such as witches, ghosts and vampires. If you love to read about creepy things and you're looking for some juicy history on the ghoulish day we call Halloween, then you've come to the right place. Here is all you need to know about the history of Halloween:

What does ‘Halloween' mean?

Halloween may sound familiar, but perhaps you never actually knew what it meant. Well, there are two theories regarding the origin of the name. First, the name “Halloween” may have its origins in the Gaelic words “Samhain” (pronounced “sow-inn”) or “Hallows” Eve. In Gaelic, Samhain is the name of the festival that falls on the last day of October, when people would light bonfires, wear costumes, and foretell the future by reading omens in the flames. “Hallows” Eve was the day before Samhain, celebrated by gathering crops, slaughtering livestock, and preparing for winter. The other theory is that the word “Halloween” is a contraction of the words “All Hallows' Eve”, meaning “All Saints' Eve” (Halloween wasn't always celebrated on October 31). “All Hallows' Eve” is the night before November 1, which is also known as “All Hallows' Day”. All Hallows' Day is a Christian religious festival that celebrates saints, who are people believed to have led lives of such religious excellence that they are now in Heaven.

Pagan Origins of Halloween

For centuries, Halloween has been a time to celebrate the dead. But the original celebrations were quite different from today's popular customs. Halloween's roots lie in Celtic (Irish and Scottish) traditions. In Celtic mythology, Samhain (pronounced “sow-in”) marks the end of the harvest and the beginning of winter. It's believed that during this time, the boundaries between the world of the living and the dead are at their thinnest. On the night of October 31, people celebrated the feast of Samhain by lighting bonfires, wearing costumes, carving turnips into lanterns, and visiting friends and neighbours. They also left offerings of food, drinks, and gifts for the “spirits”. Over time, the “spirits” they were celebrating became connected with several Christian saints' feast days. They are now associated with All Hallows' Eve (October 31) and All Saints' Day (November 1).

The Dark History of Halloween

In addition to its historical roots, Halloween has also been associated with witchcraft and other practices harmful to the health and well-being of the masses. In the early 17th century, the British Parliament passed the Witchcraft Act, making it illegal to celebrate with such rituals. With the onset of the Industrial Revolution in the late 18th century, Halloween's connection to witchcraft and paganism began to fall out of favour. It was during this time that the holiday's focus shifted from celebrating the harvest to children dressing up in costumes to beg for treats like candy from neighbours. Interestingly, Halloween celebrations were also common in the United States during the colonial era, though these were more likely to be associated with harvest-related customs and the children's folk game of trick-or-treating.

Trick or Treat: The Tradition of Halloween

One of the most popular Halloween traditions is trick-or-treating. Although the exact origins of the ritual are unknown, many historians believe that Halloween was first celebrated in ancient Ireland, where it was customary for people to go door-to-door collecting food and money. In the 12th and 13th centuries, the Church attempted to put an end to the tradition. Then, in the late 19th century, the tradition was revived in America by candy companies and advertisers who wanted to create a new holiday focused on the purchase and consumption of sweet treats. Today, the tradition of trick-or-treating among children involves dressing up in spooky costumes, going door-to-door with a bag or bucket, and asking neighbours for candy.

Why We Celebrate Halloween?

On one hand, Halloween can be seen as a celebration of the harvest. Historically, people collected crops and vegetables during the autumn, which resulted in a plentiful crop for the following year. In the present day, people mark the occasion by decorating their houses with autumnal decorations. Some people also choose to create pumpkin carvings, while others take part in community charity events. In general, Halloween has become a time for people to enjoy themselves, while also celebrating the changing seasons and the harvest. The other reason behind Halloween's popularity is that it marks the beginning of the spooky season. Halloween has become a popular time for horror films and TV shows to be released, and the spooky nature of the event makes it a perfect time for people to enjoy their favourite scary stories.

Happy, and safe Halloween!

Halloween is a celebration of the harvest, but it has also become a popular time for horror films and TV shows to be released. The spooky nature of the event makes it a perfect time for people to enjoy their favourite scary stories, and it's also a great time to decorate your house with all sorts of autumnal decorations. For most people, Halloween is a lighthearted holiday where kids go door-to-door trick-or-treating with costumes such as witches, ghosts, and vampires. But there is also a darker side to the day, with some people choosing to dress up as terrifying zombies or psychopathic killers.

The Story of Lizzie Borden: Inside the Grizzly Truth

By Staff

Lizzie Borden is one of the most infamous figures in American history. Her story has been told and re-told for over a century, with each new version more lurid than the last. In 1892, Lizzie was tried and acquitted for the axe murders of her father and stepmother in Fall River, Massachusetts. She remained in the same house, under the watchful eye of family members, until she moved out at age 42 to live with her friend Emma in another part of town. This article presents facts about Lizzie Borden's life that you probably won't read anywhere else.

Who was Lizzie Borden?

Lizzie Drew Borden (1860-1927) was a real person and not just a colorful legend. She was born in Fall River, Massachusetts. She was the second of three daughters of Andrew Borden and his second wife, Abby Durfee Gray Borden. Lizzie was a member of the large and socially prominent Borden family of Fall River, Massachusetts. Her grandfather had come from New York City to Massachusetts, where he founded a successful textile business. Lizzie's father, Andrew Borden, inherited the family business and became a wealthy and prominent member of the community. He was a member of the town council and a trustee of the local public library. He was known as an austere and disliked man. Lizzie's mother Abby was a quiet, stay-at-home woman, devoted to her husband and children.

Lizzie's Life Before the Murders

The Borden household was an unhappy one. The parents quarreled frequently, and the father was a harsh and demanding parent. He was quick to find fault with his daughters, particularly Lizzie. Although Lizzie was the oldest child and had been dutiful and obedient as a child, she was a different sort of person as a young adult. Lizzie had many admirers and several romances, but she was not interested in marriage. She was far more interested in being independent and having a career. Lizzie was frustrated by being a woman in that era. She had done very well in high school and even took a special, advanced course of study at the local business college, but she was not allowed to attend college. Lizzie wanted to become a doctor, but that was extremely rare for a woman in that era. Lizzie's father controlled her very closely and was determined that his daughters not marry or have a career. He refused to allow Lizzie to leave the house or even to have a friend come to visit, even though Lizzie's two older sisters were allowed more freedom. Lizzie did not get along with her stepmother, and the two quarreled frequently.

What Happened on the Day of the Murders?

On the morning of August 4, 1892, Lizzie's friend Nance O'Neill dropped by the Borden house to visit her. She noticed that Lizzie's car was not in the garage, which was unusual because Lizzie's father always expected his daughters to drive him to his various business appointments. Lizzie's stepmother was also absent from the house, but Lizzie herself was inside, washing the windows. Nance and Lizzie chatted for a while, and Nance noticed that Lizzie was particularly cheerful. "Something has happened," thought Nance. Nance left the house and went to visit a friend. Meanwhile, Lizzie's older sister Emma came home for lunch and found Lizzie washing the windows again. This struck Emma as odd because Lizzie had been doing this chore for weeks and was behind in her other household duties. Emma also noticed that Lizzie's shoes were covered with freshly fallen red ashes from the fireplace.

The Trial of Lizzie Borden

After the murders, the police arrested Lizzie and Emma on suspicion of the crime. Due to a lack of evidence, the police soon released Emma and focused their attention on Lizzie. Lizzie was a very calm and collected person. She was a devout Christian and went to church every Sunday. She told the police that she had stayed home alone on the day of the murders while Emma was at a birthday party at her uncle's house. Lizzie's calm demeanor and her pious and reserved lifestyle made her an unlikely suspect, but the police went ahead with the case anyway. Lizzie's lawyers had no choice but to base the case on an alibi: Emma was not with her uncle on the day of the murders and the two of them were in the same place at the same time. Lizzie's trial was a sensation, with huge crowds gathering to root for or against her.

The Aftermath of the Trial

The jury returned a verdict of Not Guilty. Lizzie was not a very popular person, but the crowds outside her house were not angry mobs bent on revenge, as Lizzie had feared they would be. Lizzie and Emma went to visit friends in New York City, and the press followed them there. Although the newspapers were critical of Lizzie, they were much more vicious to Emma, who was portrayed as a cheap and common woman who had snared a wealthy man. Lizzie was not able to return to her hometown because of the notoriety of the trial and the anger of her neighbors. She went to live with her sister in Massachusetts and later moved to New York City to work as a nurse. Lizzie never married and lived a quiet life, devoting herself to charitable causes.

Was it justice or a miscarriage of justice?

We'll never know for sure whether Lizzie Borden murdered her father and stepmother, or if she was just an innocent woman who got caught up in a frenzy of hatred and hysteria. Lizzie was an interesting and complex person. She was a dutiful, obedient daughter and a good friend to those who knew her well. She was also a very pious and religious person who spent much of her life helping others. She was also a very controlling, self-centered, and greedy person who was devoted to her interests. Lizzie's story is a cautionary tale about the dangers of jumping to conclusions based on rumor, gossip, and circumstantial evidence. It is also a reminder that a person's true nature is not always evident in a moment of excitement or challenge.

The Phantom Serial Killer: Jack the Ripper

By Staff

In the 19th century, Whitechapel was a poor and crime-ridden district in London. Its inhabitants were mostly immigrants, especially from Eastern Europe and Scotland. Also known as “the conspiracy theorists' paradise”, it has also been labeled by some as the home of the most notorious serial killer in history: Jack the Ripper. Since then, many books have been written about this mysterious figure and his crimes. So let's take a closer look at the case of Jack the Ripper: who he really is and if he ever existed or not.

Who was Jack the Ripper?

The term “Jack the Ripper” was a journalistic invention, used to describe this unknown killer. It is believed that he was the culprit behind the murders of five prostitutes in the Whitechapel area of London, between August and November 1888. Although his real name is unknown, the killer's modus operandi and some characteristics attributed to him are enough to classify him as a serial killer. His first victim was Mary Ann “Polly” Nichols, who was murdered in the early hours of August 31, 1888. Her throat was cut and abdominal mutilations were performed. Annie Chapman was killed just two days later. Her body was found by police in the backyard of 29 Hanbury Street. Mutilations were made on her abdomen, her uterus was removed and her abdominal organs were cut and arranged around the abdominal cavity. The third victim, Elizabeth Stride, was killed on the 30th of September. No mutilations were found on her body, but her throat was cut so deeply that her head was almost severed from her torso. Finally, on the 9th of October, the body of Catherine Eddowes was found in Mitford's Yard, in the Western Metropolitan City and on the same day, the last victim, Mary Jane Rivett, was found dead in the room of one of her employers, Frances Coles. Her throat was also cut and, like the other three women, her abdomen was also sliced and her uterus removed.

Facts and Evidence

The main points that have led historians to suggest that there was possibly only one murderer and that he was not identified are as follows:

- All five murders occurred in the Whitechapel district of London, within the space of three months and all during the night (between August 31st and November 9th), and at intervals of about two weeks, the first and the last being on a Sunday.

- All the women were prostitutes who were murdered in the same manner, by the removal of portions of the abdominal organs and with the same or a similar instrument.

- The mutilations were of a nature unusual in the case of such persons, and were performed with skill and in a way that was not designed as a means of removing the victim's life, but, as if for the gratification of blood lust, the selection of the particular parts removed being almost ludicrously remote from any purpose of utility from a surgical point of view.

- The discovery of the bodies was left to chance, the police being called only after the fact, and in two cases not for two days after. The portions of the bodies removed were removed with skill as if done by one used to working in a trade that requires surgical precision.

- The fact that the first and last murders were on Sundays, while the intermediate murders took place on Saturday, Wednesday and Friday.

- The fact that the murders occurred in the same locality, that the bodies were found in the same place, and that they were found in a state of decomposition.

- The fact that the murders were never reported to the police by passerby.

- The fact that in four cases the persons murdered left the homes of their own accord and in the fifth case a woman was murdered in the room of her employer.

- The fact that no weapons were found at the scenes of the murder, nor were any blood-stained garments discovered, and that no one was seen entering or leaving the premises.

- The fact that no traces were found of anyone having been drawn to the place by the cries of the victims.

In the end...

All these points indicate that these murders were the work of one person. The murderer's modus operandi was always the same and the murders were always carried out in the same fashion and with the same instrument. The murderer was always careful not to leave any evidence that could lead to his arrest. What is more, nothing links these murders to one another. All the victims were different and there were no apparent connections between them. In addition, the murderer was not seen by anyone and no traces were found of his presence at the crime scenes.

The Top 10 Creepiest Places on Earth

By Staff

Imagine a small village with cobbled streets and gothic architecture, tucked away in remote mountains. It's old, quiet... and completely abandoned. There are no signs of struggle. Nothing has been stolen or damaged as if the residents simply packed up their belongings one day and left without explanation. The creepiest places on Earth have these elements, and so much more. From hotels to prisons, there are plenty of hidden locations that seem to be teeming with paranormal activity. Why else would they be closed off to visitors? These are the creepiest places on Earth because they have everything we love about ghost stories: history, mystery, and plenty of unanswered questions.

Cardwell Asylum

This haunted asylum in Queensland, Australia, was built in 1896 and closed in 1978. It's now a tourist attraction, but it's not for the faint of heart. The hospital was nicknamed the “lunatic asylum” because it was the first mental health facility in the state. In the early days of its operation, it housed hundreds of patients in squalid conditions. There are claims that patients died from cholera, typhoid, and an outbreak of influenza. The building was used as a military hospital during both world wars. It's reported that during World War II, Japanese prisoners were treated at the building. The ghost of a Japanese soldier has been spotted at the asylum. Visitors have reported hearing screams and cries, as well as feeling a sense of dread. They've seen apparitions of bloodstains, as well as a weeping woman who was a patient at the asylum.

Bhangarh Fort

This 17th-century fort in India is the site of macabre tales of murder and magic. It's believed that a magician cursed the land of Bhangarh, and the locals killed him for his dark spells. Since then, Bhangarh has been plagued by paranormal activity. Visitors have reported hearing a woman wailing and a man shouting. The most famous Bhangarh story is that of the “doomed prince.” When a boy was born to the royal family, the king and his advisers heard a disembodied voice cry, “This child will die when he reaches the age of 18!” The king was undeterred and made the baby his heir. The boy was raised inside the walls of Bhangarh, but on his 18th birthday, a strange man appeared and informed him that he was the king's son. The prince decided to go live with his biological family and left Bhangarh. On his way out, he was struck by lightning. The town has been abandoned ever since.

Borley Asylum

Built-in 1898, this asylum was shut down in 1938 after being deemed “unfit for the reception of patients.” Four years later, it was demolished. A new building was constructed on the site, but that was torn down in 2009, leaving the grounds eerily empty. The Borley Haunted Asylum has long been considered one of the most haunted places in the world. The building was believed to be so haunted that the government paid a medium to exorcise it. Visitors have reported hearing the sounds of a woman crying, a baby crying, or a woman screaming. The spirits of two people are believed to have stayed behind. A visitor reported being grabbed and shoved, as well as being slapped in the face. The ghost of an inmate believed to have been murdered by his fellow patients has been spotted.

Changi Prison

Built by the Japanese during World War II, this prison held more than 100,000 Allied soldiers. The conditions were horrid, and many prisoners died from starvation and disease. Once the war was over, the Allies took control of the prison. Today, Changi Prison is a tourist attraction. Visitors can take tours of this infamous building. Those who take the tour have reported feeling a heavy and depressing atmosphere. They've also reported feeling as if they were being watched. Some have even claimed to have seen apparitions in the form of disembodied heads. Others have reported feeling a chilling sensation as if someone had walked over their grave.

Dom hall of Mawhrunkwah

This Balinese temple was once home to a priest and his family. One day, the priest discovered that his wife had been having an affair. Outraged, he killed both his wife and her lover. When he realized what he'd done, he committed suicide by drinking poison. The family's spirits are said to haunt the temple. Visitors have reported seeing figures walking around. Some have reported hearing a woman screaming and a baby crying. People have reported being scratched and having their hair pulled by the spirits of the priest and his wife. Others have reported smelling the stench of rotting flesh.

Ghost Island of Okinoshima

This Japanese island has been abandoned for centuries. It's believed that an ancient civilization lived on the island. They're thought to have vanished into thin air, leaving behind artifacts such as pottery and buildings. Modern-day explorers have reported seeing strange lights and hearing voices.
